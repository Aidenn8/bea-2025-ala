Abstract

We present our submission to Track 1 (Mistake Identification) of the BEA 2025 Shared Task on Pedagogical Ability Assessment of AI Tutors. Our system uses a fine-tuned bert-base-uncased model to classify tutor responses into three pedagogical categories: "Yes", "To some extent", and "No". Inputs are constructed by concatenating the dialogue history with the current tutor response, allowing the model to assess whether the tutor accurately identifies student mistakes. Trained on the 2,476-response development set, our final submission achieved a strict macro-F1 score of 0.6361 and a lenient macro-F1 score of 0.8493 on the official test set, placing our team at 28th position in the shared task leaderboard.

Methodology

Model Architecture

Fine-tuned bert-base-uncased transformer with a linear classification head for three-class prediction: "Yes", "To some extent", and "No" categories for pedagogical assessment.

Input Processing

Concatenated dialogue history with current tutor response, tokenized with 512-token limit and truncation applied from the beginning to preserve recent context.

Training Configuration

AdamW optimizer with learning rate 1×10⁻⁵, batch size 16, trained for 4 epochs with 0.01 weight decay on single NVIDIA T4 GPU.

Dataset Composition

2,476 AI tutor responses from MathDial and Bridge datasets with stratified 80/20 train/validation split for robust evaluation.

Key Findings & Analysis

Competitive Baseline Performance

Despite minimal feature engineering, our BERT-based approach achieved competitive results with a substantial performance gap between strict (0.6361) and lenient (0.8493) F1 scores, indicating challenges in precise boundary classification.

Borderline Classification Challenges

Most classification errors occurred between "Yes" and "To some extent" categories, highlighting the inherent difficulty in making nuanced pedagogical judgments and the subjective nature of educational feedback assessment.

Error Pattern Analysis

Three primary failure modes identified: (1) tone bias leading to "Yes" overprediction, (2) confusion with implicit pedagogical guidance, and (3) systematic underprediction of "No" labels in clear negative cases.

Model Generalization

Strong correlation between development and test set performance suggests good generalization capabilities, with consistent performance patterns across both evaluation metrics and datasets.
