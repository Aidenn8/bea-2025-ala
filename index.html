Abstract

We present our submission to Track 1 (Mistake Identification) of the BEA 2025 Shared Task on Pedagogical Ability Assessment of AI Tutors. Our system uses a fine-tuned bert-base-uncased model to classify tutor responses into three pedagogical categories: "Yes", "To some extent", and "No". Inputs are constructed by concatenating the dialogue history with the current tutor response, allowing the model to assess whether the tutor accurately identifies student mistakes. Trained on the 2,476-response development set, our final submission achieved a strict macro-F1 score of 0.6361 and a lenient macro-F1 score of 0.8493 on the official test set.

Method Overview

Model Architecture

Fine-tuned BERT-base-uncased with a linear classification head for three-class prediction: "Yes", "To some extent", and "No".

Input Processing

Concatenated dialogue history with tutor response, tokenized with 512-token limit and truncation from the beginning.

Training Setup

AdamW optimizer, 1e-5 learning rate, batch size 16, 4 epochs with 0.01 weight decay on single NVIDIA T4 GPU.

Dataset

2,476 AI tutor responses from MathDial and Bridge datasets with 80/20 stratified train/validation split.

Key Findings

Strong Baseline Performance

Despite minimal engineering, achieved competitive results with substantial gap between strict (0.6361) and lenient (0.8493) F1 scores.

Borderline Case Challenges

Most errors occurred between "Yes" and "To some extent" categories, indicating difficulty with nuanced pedagogical judgment.

Error Pattern Analysis

Three main failure modes: tone bias leading to "Yes" overprediction, confusion with implicit guidance, and underprediction of "No" labels.
